Clinical Predictors of Coccidioidomycosis 

Background. Coccidioidomycosis (CM), colloquially known as “cocci” or “Valley fever”, is a fungal infection endemic to the southwestern United States and parts of Central and South America.(1) Infection occurs through inhalation of an arthroconidium from the dimorphic, soil dwelling fungi Coccidioides immitis and Coccidioides posadasii. Due to limited ability to prevent CM exposure in the community and no existing vaccine, CM poses a substantial burden to patients and healthcare systems in endemic areas. CM’s increased incidence and complex presentations emphasize the need to improve disease identification in clinical settings.

Objective. We developed a prediction model for CM based on demographic, clinical, and laboratory factors. Models were developed for outpatient and inpatient settings independently.

Methods. We first developed univariable logistic regression models, reporting all parameter estimates in terms of odds ratios on imputed data. Multivariable models were constructed using the semi-automated least absolute shrinkage and selection (LASSO) method on imputed data. We used the mean of these two lambda values to penalize our models. Variables with non-zero coefficients were retained in each model. We selected LASSO due to the ability to select influential features in a high-dimensional dataset (i.e., a high number of variables relative to the dataset). Other regression methods often suffer degeneracies when the number of predictors exceeds or is close to the number of observations.
